Sự khó khăn của good faith là những người tốt thực sự không bao giờ cố gắng làm việc tốt để trở thành người tốt. Mặc dù đúng là họ sẽ trở thành người tốt.

Hoặc nhân danh đạo đức để thực hiện hành vi vô đạo đức cũng là đạo đức giả. Nhóm này có thể tự nhận biết bản thân là người như thế, hoặc không.

Những người đạo đức giả thường rao giảng những gì đúng và được chấp nhận là đạo đức bởi đa số, nhưng họ không làm thế. Thậm chí họ còn bắt người khác phải thực hiện hành vi đạo đức, bằng một ép buộc vô đạo đức.

Ví dụ, những người không từ thiện (hoặc từ thiện rất ít) chửi bới những người từ thiện ít khác, hay như Hitler nhân danh những giá trị hắn cho là tốt nhất để ép mọi người phải thực hiện theo một cách vô đạo đức… đều là đạo đức giả.

Xã hội chúng ta không thiếu những người cực đoan, với nền tảng đạo đức yếu ớt, nhưng luôn ép người này phải làm thế này, người kia phải làm thế kia theo ý họ. Điều này thực sự rất khó chịu. Bọn họ cứ cư xử như thể mình biết tất cả mọi thứ, và chỉ cần làm theo họ là thế giới sẽ trở nên tốt đẹp. Điều buồn cười là, những người hiểu chuyện thực sự không bao giờ cư xử như thế.

Với những người này, chỉ cần một vài câu hỏi đơn giản như “sao bạn không làm đi?” là đủ để vạch mặt. Tuy vậy, đôi lúc câu hỏi này cũng ảnh hưởng đến những người có good faith nhưng ngây thơ.

Chẳng hạn, Monster Box thường xuyên đối mặt với việc có nhiều người bình luận trên YouTube rằng “sao không làm tiếng Việt, tiếng Việt giờ nghèo nàn đến vậy sao?”, “kênh Việt Nam làm cho người Việt đi làm cho bọn nước ngoài xem làm gì”... mặc dù nghe như nhân danh đem lại lợi ích cho nước nhà, nhưng thực chất là đạo đức giả. Vì sao bạn không làm, hay ít nhất, sao bạn không cho chúng tôi tiền để làm đi?

Có một số khác cũng đến tận nơi khuyên chúng tôi hãy làm thế này đi, làm thế kia đi, nhưng có thể cảm nhận được good faith (họ không thể làm được, chỉ là muốn những người làm được có thể làm giúp họ) dù lời khuyên của họ có chút áp đặt. Hi vọng good faith sẽ giúp họ thay đổi được tật xấu này, hoặc không, tức là họ vốn có bad faith.

Chúng tôi là những người có thành ý, và luôn như vậy. Các bạn có thể cảm nhận thông qua việc theo dõi Monster Box từ trước đến nay. Chúng tôi có sai lầm, có sửa chữa, có xin lỗi và luôn hướng đến lợi ích chung cũng nhờ vào động cơ từ đầu trước sau như một. Nếu không, có lẽ mọi thứ chẳng được như hôm nay.

Qua quá trình làm việc, có một lời khuyên, một bài học ngắn chúng tôi tự đúc kết được:

“Nói những điều tốt đẹp, nghĩ những điều tốt đẹp không khiến bạn tốt đẹp lên. Vì bạn không hề biết những gì mình nói hay nghĩ có thực sự tốt đẹp không, hay chỉ là những ẩn ức thấp hèn. Nhưng với một động cơ thuần khiết, cùng những hành động cụ thể, rồi bạn sẽ dần nhận ra liệu mình có phải người tốt hay không. Bí quyết là phải luôn có hành động cụ thể”.

Bí quyết là phải luôn có hành động cụ thể.

GOOD FAITH, CHARITY, AND “WHY AIN’T YOU DO IT?”

I often ask my friends whether who amongst the three subjects, they reckon, shall be the most conducive to the society:

A philanthropist whose cordiality and sincerity are preeminent, life always tilting toward charity, and indeed have donated a handsome amount to charity funds.

A celebrity thriving on scandals, who also attempts to do charitable works, having raised large charitable sums. Everyone, nonetheless, knows all too well that a behavior as such is purely to gain him some fame.

An ordinary, kind and sincere individual who partially contributes to charitable funds. His contribution, thus, is constrained to negligible amounts only.

Undoubtedly, every respondent immediately went for the first. I, however, always prompted another question: leaving aside their choice, there are now only 2 options they could opt for. This time, it took them some awkward pauses before coming with the common, predestined decision: the second.

Further inquired of why, every of whom (again woefully common) responded that what matters, after all, is that the celebrity has contributed [woefully more] to the society.

1. Good faith

This week's theme is “The Micro,” which is all about microscopic things that, nevertheless, inflict onerous impacts on the macro. Let alone natural sciences topics, such as the DNA structure or the world at atomic level, social science still holds every, albeit elemental, rarely-properly-educated concept, thus engendering heaps of cataclysms. A concrete example of which is good faith.

Back to the personal story of mine, we indeed are fervently extolling the end-products whilst despising the so-called “process”. In all likelihood, many are reckoning that only those end-products, neither the in-between processes nor the motivations, exert some actual impacts on reality. For example, a warm-hearted guy who is to help an oldster cross the street, given his clumsiness, runs the latter into a traffic accident. Irregardless of his every motivation, by no means could it deny the fact that the oldster abears the consequences thereafter.

By the same token, an entrepreneur washing his dirty money by charitable works, albeit illegal, could never rebuff that thousands have been saved since.

After all, we are coming to the very conclusion that the good guy is evil instead, whilst the entrepreneur is scrupulous indeed.

But is it the very patterns of reality? What, then, would be the values (if any) of the moral lessons we are all too heard of - other than to be shucked?

Otherwise, we must have always done critical thinking falsifiably. We, after all, have been all too naive to presume that this life is solely a point - instead of a straight line leading all the eay to infinity.

That kind-hearted guy would be wicked, were he to never exist; so is the entrepreneur, who would only be a morally sound individual, were everything he does donating dirty money to charity, saving thousands of his fellow-humans. Which are, to all appearances, woefully absurd for life is inherently made up of series after series of ensuing events that humans, nevertheless, often let slip when it comes to unusual, individual circumstances.

We, for the most part, are getting this wrong. Akin to gamblers, we always think of each bet as a “serendipity” that has solely two outcomes: to win, or to lose; thus incognizant that the end-products of which are under the impacts of overlapping factors, alongside sequences of preceding events.

To the bookies (thus broader), they, however, never mind the outcome of such single games. Their only task is to cunningly create games with 51% of gamblers’ losing odds so that they always win by a margin of 1% at the end of the day, whilst the players always lose 1% respectively. Every bookmaker, thus, never cheats, their all-time job is to suck in enough players, who in turn would play for long enough to assure that 1% margin alone is a fortune.

Now let us zoom in life as a day in a casino - with single events being gambling - while we play the visitors. First, we head towards the roulette table, witnessing with our bare eyes that the dealer is losing - akin to the blackjack table or the slot machine, where players are actually winning. After all, is the bookmaker de facto losing? Let us, at this point, concede to the fact that we are, and will never heed the final consequences of which.

On the whole, no visitor would travel around the casino all day to calculate the bookie’s profits, since oodles of casino events during that day, as a rule, would coerce them to miss most of which. All they are seeing, thus, is only the tip of the iceberg, given that homing in the minority to yield an overall assessment is never a good way.

Akin to life, wherein the number of events we are actually exposed to is woefully less than those we ain’t. In this manner, is zeroing on what we vision (the tiny little tip), to leap to conclusion somewhat a prudent, human behavior?

No, forsooth.

In fact, we, nonetheless, are prominent species who always make cautious judgments from a variety of facts, alongside our human intuitions. That reasons out our sympathy for the kind-hearted goofy moviresque characters, however cataclysmic their kind-intended actions are; together with our nauseation against the evil, however painstakingly it is disguised to swindle the rest of that moviesque world.

Since we can still see it [intentions], we know all too well that even though the dull, albeit spoiling everything, still hold the purity that may offer tremendous help to others (oftentimes, most movies demonstrate this ideology all too actively). On the other hand, as audiences, since we are heeding every unscrupulous behavior, even though the protagonists only see the contrary, it is deemed effortless to leap to our own conclusions.

So is life. The only difference is that we are now playing the movie characters - those holding by no means sufficient access to information.

After all, the aforementioned guy’s acquaintances may sympathize with him, since they know horribly well that he is indeed a moral being, with good intentions towards others. And they reckon he would, as usual. Strangers, on the other hand, do not. By no means would they heed such a being (unless his story is appealing enough).

Meanwhile, the dirty well-heeled who are hoodwinking the public, however much he donates to charitable funds, would never win back any reverences from his acquaintances, who, in turn, know all too well that every such sublime work is to camouflage his previous cunning schemes, and that a such tiger never changes its stripes.

Akin to the afore-argued casino visit, any prominent-enough person would heed that a loss round, to bookmakers, never foretells their loss at the end of the day. So are players’ winning streaks.

If we, after all, must heed the bookies’ winning rate in their casinos, what else should we keep tabs on amidst everyday life?

Good, and bad faith.

Since we are always in a state of inadequate access to information, to zoom in anyone, let us pay every attention to the motives instead of his appearance (or leveraging which in scrutinizing motives - the ultimate subjects).

After all, a scrupulous man, given his good faith, alongside the high attempts to back others, may not yield good results (seldom is he in control of every external factor). Nevertheless, some seemingly dismal situations would never distort the fact that he has, and will perpetuate his moral behaviors. From a social perspective, he, thus, is a moral being.

The unscrupulous well-heeled, given the bad faith, would never do away his previous criminals. However handsomely he is seemingly contributing to the community, he still can rarely assure a favorable outcome. Also, he never knows if such charitable works would breed more corruptions instead.

So are single events. For example, in a debate, the good-faith participants may either swat at others, articulate something drab, fallaciously argue, yet none of which could deny the fact that they are with good will, and are doing everything to accomplish such goals. On the contrary, those who have amoral motives, albeit voicing every sublime speech, may not necessarily engender any common good, since their purposes are inherently not to head towards which.

After all, we can always place our best bets on those with good will, or at least good purposes, for they always promptly adjust their behaviors, heed others’ opinions, and stay open so that everything would happen in alignment with their original purposes.

Perhaps this might explain why Buddhism forgives those having committed every evil deed, as long as they repent and will to change, since such transitions are brought about from changes to one’s nature and motivations, instead of superficial, surface changes (still my personal deduction, after all).

That said, by no means is this world simply black-and-white, nor are humans born hard-wired to good/bad faith. What we should aim for, also the most elemental moral lesson, is to gravitate towards good faith in any conversation.

To all appearances, this world is still vehemently longing for the mighty good faith capable of accomplishing good things (aka the first character in the opening story), alongside constraints to the unscrupulous always tilting towards bad things. Fortunately enough, such characters only account for the minority in the society. We, for the most part, are the “hybrid”: yielding ill-favored outcomes from good faith, or luck out of bad faith.

The conventional social morality and welfare rule that we should morph in the former group.

2. "Why ain't you do it?" and the ultra-extremists

Good faith, after all, is a far cry from "self-assuring that such a thing is for good faith". The fact that people with good motivations oftentimes spoil everything does never imply that those spoiling things are bearing with good motives. Simply put, those calling for the good may still run out of goodwill.

Most recently, there have been cursing waves at celebrities: someone fiercely criticized Thuy Tien upon showing off her mattress (in a mattress ads video), asserting that her “job” is to do charitable works. Others even swatted at H’Hen Niê for having donated “solely” 50 million. Seemingly charity-related, which is scrupulous, by no means are those callers always with good faith.

Someone might blaspheme others purely to polish their “good faith” (bad faith), to voice their resentment (bad faith), or to manifest hostility on the victims (bad faith). Every so often, some are even so eccentric to self-assure that "since I’m thinking of others’ good, I must be a fervently moral being" (bad faith also).

The good faith’s paradox, after all, is that the truly moral never have to strive for good deeds to be considered righteous. Even though it seems inescapable that they will, forsooth.

Every society always abears a certain number of hypocrites, the more fragile the former is, the more fervently the latter thrive. Albeit always with bad faith, this group, nonetheless, is prominent at “duplicating” good faith to win back social recognition (every so often self-recognition), or blowing their “moral horns” whilst committing immoral acts. Those involved may still be heedless of which.

Hypocrites often preach what is deemed conventionally right and moral by the majority, given that theirs are not. Worse still, they might even coercively urge others to commit moral behaviors.

For example, those rarely doing charitable works (or little, if any), yet fiercely criticizing other like-minds, or Hitler, who on behalf of his self-acclaimed moral values, coercing others, are all hypocrites.

Our society has never run short of extremists, who, albeit with frail moral foundations, always force others to behave in accordance with their will. Which must be woefully frustrating. To unmask these c*nts, such child-like questions as "why ain't you do it?" is enough, even though any of which may spoil those with good faith.

To demonstrate, Monster Box often runs into such typical YouTube comments as "why ain't your videos in Vietnamese, is our language that cheap?", "why is a Vietnamese channel making international videos [in English]”. Patriotic as it might sound, every of which is de facto hypocritical. Why ain’t you do it on your own; or at least, invest in some money, fuelling the company?

There are, however, others who face-to-face coercively advise us to do in accordance with their wills. At those, we still can feel the good faith (given their incapability, they vehemently hurt for others fueling their dreams), irregardless of the coercion in their tones. To date, we still hope that such good faith would eventually do away with the overwhelming bad faith. Otherwise, we would have no other choice but concede to the fact that they do hold some bad faith.

We are always with good faith, which you, readers, may easily spell out from our works. We did commit mistakes, correct them, apologize for which, and always aim at the ultimate common good, thanks to the analogous motivations since our beginning. Otherwise, there could have been no such Monster Box.

Given our [somewhat veteran] working process, here we are with one piece of advice for all of us:

“Seldom does saying, or bearing good things polish your personality. Never could you know whether such self-assuring goodness is indeed scrupulous, or purely some latent resentment. On the contrary, given a pure motivation, alongside every prompt action, you would piece by piece unveil your true self. The ‘tip’ is to always take prompt action”.

Always, take prompt action.
